## 🧭 Engineering Philosophy
I build software for environments where correctness, clarity, and predictability matter. Most of my work lives in high‑reliability industrial and semiconductor automation systems, where a single timing issue or ambiguous requirement can ripple through an entire production line. That experience shaped how I think about engineering:
- **Clarity over cleverness** — Code should be readable, intentional, and easy to reason about years later.  
- **Design before implementation** — Good diagrams, sequence flows, and edge‑case analysis prevent entire classes of bugs.  
- **Maintainability as a requirement** — Long‑lifecycle systems demand code that can evolve safely.  
- **Defensive programming** — Real hardware behaves unpredictably; software should not.  
- **Thoughtful communication** — Cross‑disciplinary work succeeds when information is shared clearly and early.  
- **Continuous improvement** — Small refinements compound into meaningful reliability gains.  
I care deeply about building systems that “just work” — not because they’re simple, but because they’re designed with intention.

## 🧩 How I Approach Complex Problems
My work often involves ambiguity, timing‑dependent behavior, and hardware/software interactions. Over time, I’ve developed a consistent approach to tackling complex problems:
### **1. Understand the feature, use-cases and workflow end‑to‑end**  
I start by mapping the full workflow for the feature I’m working on — the relevant client motivation, hardware behaviour, software events, timing constraints, and failure modes. Even in large legacy systems where full system knowledge isn’t realistic, understanding the boundaries and interactions of the feature helps me identify risks, dependencies, and where timing or behavior might be affected.
### **2. Clarify requirements early**  
I work closely with process engineers, hardware teams, product teams, and other developers to remove ambiguity. Clear requirements reduce rework and make design decisions defensible.
### **3. Break the problem into predictable components**  
I identify what must be reliable, what must be fast, and what must be flexible. This informs where to invest in robustness, observability, or abstraction.
### **4. Design with timing and real‑world behavior in mind**  
Hardware latency, sequencing, and physical constraints matter as much as software execution time. I incorporate these into my design decisions from the start.
### **5. Build tools that make the system easier to reason about**  
Simulators, logs, graphs, and diagnostic utilities often reveal patterns that raw data hides. I use these to validate assumptions and guide iteration.
### **6. Iterate thoughtfully**  
I prefer small, verifiable steps — especially when working with hardware or distributed components. Each iteration should reduce uncertainty.
### **7. Communicate clearly and often**  
Complex problems require alignment. I share findings, risks, and design decisions early so teams can move together.
### **8. Reflect and generalize**  
After solving a hard problem, I look for patterns we can reuse: abstractions, base classes, scheduling improvements, or design principles that apply to future tools.
This approach has helped me deliver reliable systems under tight timelines, navigate ambiguity, and collaborate effectively across disciplines.
